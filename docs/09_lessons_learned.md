# 9. Lessons Learned

## 1. AI adoption is an operating-model challenge

Model capability matters, but repeatable value depends on ownership, workflow design, controls, user behaviour and measurement.

## 2. Permission design is more important than agent count

Adding more agents does not automatically improve quality. Clear roles, read boundaries, separation of duties and escalation rules are more valuable than unrestricted autonomy.

## 3. Human approval should be designed, not assumed

“Human in the loop” is weak unless the human has defined evidence, decision rights and enough time to challenge the output.

## 4. Fail-closed behaviour builds trust

An AI workflow should stop when authority or evidence is missing. Confident completion under ambiguity creates hidden operational risk.

## 5. Independent validation changes behaviour

When outputs are reviewed against deterministic acceptance criteria, task design becomes clearer and scope expansion becomes easier to detect.

## 6. Governance must remain usable

Excessively complex controls can drive users to unofficial workarounds. Controls should be proportional to risk and embedded into the workflow.

## 7. Adoption metrics need a baseline

Without a baseline, claimed time savings and quality improvements are difficult to verify. Measurement design belongs at pilot intake, not after launch.

## 8. Regional scale requires local ownership

A regional policy can define minimum standards, but local teams must adapt language, workflow and regulatory interpretation.

## 9. Portfolio transparency includes failures

A credible programme records stopped pilots, unmet assumptions and retired use cases. Failure to scale can be a valuable outcome when detected early.

## 10. This case study has limits

The repository presents a sanitized operating model and reusable templates. It does not prove production deployment, organisation-wide adoption or quantified business benefits. Those claims require real user, risk and outcome evidence.
