# 1. Business Problem

## The adoption gap

Generative-AI pilots often start quickly but stall before enterprise scale. The technology may work, yet the organisation lacks a repeatable way to decide:

- which use cases are worth pursuing;
- who owns the outcome;
- what data an AI workflow may access;
- when human review is mandatory;
- how quality and risk are measured;
- how local markets can adapt a regional standard;
- whether adoption creates durable business value.

## Common failure modes

| Failure mode | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Tool-first rollout | Licences are issued without workflow redesign |
| Unclear ownership | Business, technology and risk teams wait for each other |
| Inconsistent prompting | Quality depends on individual users |
| Weak controls | Sensitive data or unsupported claims enter workflows |
| No baseline | Productivity benefits cannot be proven |
| One-size-fits-all deployment | Local regulation and language needs are ignored |
| Activity-only metrics | Prompt volume is mistaken for business impact |

## Case-study challenge

The source environment involved AI-assisted work in a controlled research repository. The core challenge was not simply generating code or documents; it was creating an operating model that made AI activity:

- bounded;
- explainable;
- reviewable;
- reversible;
- auditable;
- reusable.

## Target outcome

The target state is a governed AI-adoption system where every material use case has:

1. a business owner;
2. a clear user problem;
3. a risk classification;
4. approved data boundaries;
5. a human-review design;
6. measurable outcomes;
7. an adoption and change plan;
8. an escalation path.

This case study focuses on how to build that system, not on promoting a specific model or vendor.
